# Imagery Guidelines

Imagery is essential to the Winningtemp brand. It carries the **human side** — what working life actually looks and feels like across the customers we serve.

The visual language is **honest, warm, and modern**. Photos should feel like real moments, not staged production.

## The look

### Filter-free, natural aesthetic
- Light, modern tones
- Balanced contrast (not crushed shadows or blown highlights)
- True-to-life color (no heavy color grading, no Instagram filters, no orange-and-teal)
- Soft natural light wherever possible (window light, golden hour, daylight studios)

### Cohesive but not matching
Photos in a single piece should feel like they were shot in the same world — similar light quality, similar tonal range, similar honesty. They don't need to be from the same shoot or feature the same people.

### Modern, not trendy
Avoid visual fads (heavy bokeh, vintage tones, faux-film grain). The brand should still look right in five years.

## Subject and representation

### Show the full range of customers

Winningtemp serves the **entire workforce**, not just office workers. Photography must reflect this.

Roles to represent across our materials over time:
- Office professionals (knowledge workers, managers, executives)
- Frontline / blue-collar workers (manufacturing, logistics, retail, healthcare, hospitality)
- Field / mobile workers (technicians, drivers, on-site staff)
- Hybrid teams (a mix of remote and in-office)

A single asset doesn't need to show all of these — but the **portfolio** of imagery used over a quarter or campaign should.

### Inclusivity (mandatory)
- Mix of genders
- Mix of ethnicities
- Mix of ages (not just 25–35)
- Mix of abilities where appropriate (do not segregate to a "diversity slot")
- Geographic / cultural variety, especially across our Nordic markets (Sweden, Norway) and the broader EMEA presence

Inclusivity is not a checkbox — it's just what the actual workforce looks like.

### Authenticity over staging
- Real interactions over posed handshakes
- Working environments over generic offices
- Real expressions over rehearsed smiles
- Hands-on activity over standing-and-pointing

If a photo could be a stock photo for "any SaaS company," skip it.

## What to avoid

| Don't use | Why |
|---|---|
| Heavily filtered or stylized photos | Breaks the natural aesthetic |
| Staged "diverse team huddle" stock | Reads as performative; specific real photos always beat this |
| Headshots in front of corporate gradient backgrounds | Dated; doesn't fit the editorial look |
| Photos with visible non-Winningtemp branding (other companies' logos) | Permission + brand-collision issues |
| Whiteboards with fake brainstorming notes | Feels dishonest |
| Laptop-screens-with-fake-data shots | Use real product UI screenshots instead |
| Conference room with people pointing at a chart | Cliché |
| Lone "thinking person staring out window" shot | No story, no information |
| Heavy bokeh / blurred backgrounds for everything | Trendy but doesn't fit the honest aesthetic |
| Black-and-white treatment | Wrong tonal feel for the brand |
| Photos where you can clearly see "this is Sweden" or "this is Norway" only | Limit national-coding so the same asset works across markets |

## Selection checklist

Before using a photo in any branded artifact:

```
- [ ] Light quality is natural and balanced (not heavily filtered)
- [ ] At least one person is recognizable / present (rare exception: product UI shots)
- [ ] Composition has space for text overlay (if needed) without crowding
- [ ] Subject reflects a real role, not a stock pose
- [ ] No visible third-party logos
- [ ] Crops well to required aspect (4:3, 16:9, 1:1, 4:5)
- [ ] Maintains quality at the target output size (no obvious upscaling)
- [ ] Doesn't visually clash with adjacent brand elements
```

## Sourcing

In order of preference:

1. **Approved Winningtemp image bank** — accessible via the brand team / SharePoint (see `references/external-resources.md`). Always check here first.
2. **Real customer photography** — with permission. Get written sign-off and store consent records with the asset.
3. **Branded shoots** — commissioned photography for campaigns. Use the brand team's photography brief template.
4. **Curated stock libraries** — Death to Stock, Unsplash+, Stocksy. Filter aggressively for the natural aesthetic. Many free stock sites lean too "lifestyle-blogger" — be picky.
5. **Generic free stock** (Unsplash free, Pexels) — last resort. The "anyone could be using this" risk is real.

### Never:
- Use Google Image search results
- Use AI-generated photography of "people" (uncanny issues + ethical issues with workforce-focused brand)
- Use photos sourced from social media without explicit permission
- Use stock that obviously appears in competitor materials

## Treatment in layout

How photos sit inside the brand visual system:

### Default container: rounded rectangle
- Corner radius ~5–8% of the shorter side
- No drop shadow, no border (or 1px Light Purple line if separation is needed)
- Generous white space around it
- See `references/graphic-elements.md` for full specs

### Brand-shape masks (occasional)
- For campaign / editorial moments only
- Use the inner-star or symbol mask
- One per piece — emphasis, not pattern

### Text over photography
- Add a subtle white or Deep Purple scrim/gradient if text legibility requires it
- Keep contrast ratio ≥ 4.5:1 for body, ≥ 3:1 for large type
- Headline in Poppins Semibold, body in Inter Regular

### Color overlays
- Avoid heavy color washes
- A subtle Deep Purple multiply at 20–30% can unify a mixed-light photo, but use sparingly
- Never use Wave, coral, or other accent colors as a photo overlay — keeps photos honest

## Common mistakes

1. **Defaulting to office/desk shots** when the customer is in healthcare, manufacturing, logistics, etc. Match the photo to the audience's actual work environment.
2. **Heavy filters / color grades** that look "designy" — breaks the natural aesthetic.
3. **Reusing the same hero photo** across many materials. People notice. Rotate.
4. **Using stock that has appeared on competitor sites** — easy to spot, embarrassing when noticed.
5. **Photos with cropped-out details** (half a head, half a logo) — looks unfinished.
6. **Putting black text on busy photos** without a scrim — fails contrast.
7. **Ignoring the rounded-rectangle mask** rule — sharp-corner photos clash with the brand.
